the cheapest flight is not the best flight. we learned this the expensive way: a $180 fare that landed at 11:40pm, last airport train gone, $65 taxi, both of us zombies until noon the next day. the "cheap" flight cost more than the normal one and ate half a day of the visit.

so now we score every flight on one number: **cost per hour actually together.** total door-to-door cost (fare + bags + transport + the hours you're too wrecked to function) divided by awake hours in the same room. once you rank flights that way, a lot of "deals" stop being deals.

## the only metric that matters

> 📊 $/hour together | fare + transport + zombie time, divided by awake hours in the same city

work it through once and it rewires your brain. a red-eye that saves $60 but deletes your first morning together is not saving you $60. a flight that lands at 9am on a saturday is worth paying a premium for, because it hands you two full days instead of one and a half.

> pip's take: you are not buying a seat. you are buying hours with your person. shop for the hours.

## when to book (the honest version)

there's a whole industry of "book exactly 54 days out" content and most of it is noise. the boring truth that actually works:

- **domestic australia:** fares are pretty flat until 2 to 3 weeks out, then they climb. book 3 to 8 weeks ahead and you're fine. inside 14 days is where you get punished.
- **school holidays and long weekends are the real enemy.** the calendar moves prices more than any booking-window trick. if your visit can slide one week off a holiday peak, that's routinely a 30 to 50% difference.
- **set a tracker and stop checking manually.** Google Flights price alerts on your route, both directions. let the robot watch. when it dips, book. this removes the "is now a good time??" anxiety spiral entirely.

⁠the corollary: because you're flying the *same route* over and over, you learn its rhythm fast. after three bookings you'll know what "cheap" looks like for your city pair, and you can pounce without second-guessing.

## which day, which time

- **tuesday, wednesday, saturday** departures are consistently the cheap ones. friday evening and sunday evening are the expensive ones, because that's when everyone with a normal life flies.
- if one of you can work remotely even one day, **fly thursday night or friday morning and work from their place.** you just turned a 2-day visit into a 3-day one for zero extra leave.
- **morning flights get cancelled less.** delays snowball through the day, so the 7am is the most reliable flight on the board. for a 48-hour visit, a 3-hour delay is 6% of the entire trip. protect the visit.

> ⚠️ real talk: the last flight of the day has no backup. if it's cancelled, you're not arriving until tomorrow, and that might be a quarter of your visit gone. we only book the last flight when there's literally no other option.

## budget carrier maths, without the gotchas

in australia the Jetstar-vs-the-rest question comes down to discipline:

- the Jetstar starter fare is real *if* you fly carry-on only. we keep a duplicate toothbrush-and-charger kit at each other's places so we can fly with a backpack. that's the whole trick. bags are where the "cheap" fare quietly becomes a normal-priced fare.
- add the checked bag, seat pick, and "flexibility" upsell and you've usually rebuilt a Virgin fare with worse cancellation cover. price the *final* number, not the headline.
- **one route, one airline, on purpose.** flying the same carrier every visit stacks points and status instead of scattering them. frequent visits are the one situation where loyalty programs actually pay out, because you're generating flights whether you like it or not.

> 💸 the duplicate-toiletries kit costs about $30 once and saves a checked bag basically every flight after. best ROI in the entire relationship budget.

## the scoreboard

| choice | take it when | skip it when |
|---|---|---|
| cheapest fare, awful time | you're broke this month, full stop | it deletes a morning together |
| red-eye | you sleep fine on planes (liar) | the visit is under 3 days |
| morning direct | almost always, it's the reliable one | never, honestly |
| last flight of the day | there is no other flight | a cancellation would gut the trip |
| checked bag | staying a week+ | you kept a kit at their place |

## the number behind all of this

every trick above is about making the *visits* cheaper. but the visits themselves are the recurring cost that never goes away until you close the distance for good. flights are the rent you pay on being apart.
